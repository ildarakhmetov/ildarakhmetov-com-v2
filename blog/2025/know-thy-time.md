Before you start changing your priorities, habits, or workload, it helps to take a step back. Or maybe a step *inward*.

Socrates (or maybe Plato quoting Socrates?) once said: **"Know thyself."** Great advice.

So here's my version for today:

> **Before you shift your time, track your time.**

We often have gut feelings like "I should spend more time doing X," or "I waste too much time on Y." But… how much time *exactly* do I spend on X and Y right now? Most people don't know. I didn't either — until I started tracking.

## Point Zero: Measure Before You Tweak

If you want to optimize anything — your focus, your workload, your boundaries — you need a baseline. That's **Point Zero**. No edits and no judgement, just data.

Once I had that, I could start seeing patterns. Not just "I feel busy," but:

- Am I working the right *amount* of hours?
- Am I spending them on things that matter?
- Am I doing the right mix across my roles?

For example, I'm paid to work ~40 hours per week for the university. That's a full-time job — even if I'm not teaching that week. So I want to make sure of three things:

- That I'm not dipping below 40 (underworking isn't fair to my team — or to myself),
- That I'm not regularly going over 40 (thanks to my colleague and mentor, Juancho, for reminding me it's okay to log off), and
- That within those 40 hours, I'm doing meaningful, not just reactive work. 40 hours answering email? That's a trap.

The goal isn't to squeeze more in — it's to make the hours count — so start by actually *counting* (or tracking) them!

## My Own Thresholds

Beyond the 40 for the university, I cap my total weekly work time at around **60 hours**. That includes passion projects, side learning, admin catch-up, and more. Not because 60 is a magic number — but because I learned myself over the years.

> If I go *over* 60, I'll need to recover soon. If I go *under* 60 (and it's not vacation), something's probably off — either I'm hitting a wall, coasting, or just bored.

So yes, the time log is a *signal*. It helps me ask:

- Which projects are eating up more time than they should?
- Which ones get constantly neglected, despite being important?

Sometimes the answers surprise me. And sometimes they're a nudge to act.

## Bonus: The Anti-Fake-Work Trigger

There's another small, surprising benefit:

> Tracking helps kill fake productivity.

If I'm sitting at my laptop, pretending to work but actually doomscrolling… I'll stop the timer. That small act — a physical acknowledgment — snaps me out of it. I know, research [shows](https://professional.dce.harvard.edu/blog/the-perks-of-procrastination/) that procrastination serves a psychological purpose. But it's one thing to drift, and another to make it conscious. And the timer helps.

## So... What Tool?

Years ago, I tried tracking time on paper. It was useful as a one-off, but not sustainable.

Then I found **[Toggl Track](https://toggl.com/)**, and I've stuck with it for years. It's simple. One button: Play / Stop. No automation, no browser spying, no AI guessing what you're working on. Simplicity wins.

If I go make tea, I stop. Back at my desk? Play. Even on my phone — if I'm studying for my MBA on transit, I track it. Those 30 minutes matter.

## My Categories (Yours Will Be Different)

Here's what I currently use:

- **University: Teaching**
- **University: Admin**
- **University: Research**
- **University: Strategic** (Yes, I need to know if this one disappears. Two weeks in a row with zero "Strategic"? Big red flag.)
- **Events / Networking** (Some are fully work-related, some are tangential. I lump them for now. I never track purely fun stuff — like concerts or time with my kid.)
- **Learning and Self-Growth** (This is where I log my MBA, the odd Udemy course, or a book on leadership. A light novel? Probably not. Your call.)
- **Future** (That's the category that is currently on while I'm writing this post. Coding on weekends, sketching ideas would also go here. Could be called "Passion Projects" or "Personal R&D." I like "Future".)
- **Life Ops** (Booking tickets. Doing taxes. Dealing with random adulting. Necessary, but not energizing.)

This setup works for *me*. Yours will *definitely* look different.

## Want to Try?

You don't need my exact system. You might not even need a system *yet*. But if you're feeling stretched or stuck — or you're wondering where your time *really* goes — just try this:

> For one week, track your time. Honestly. No optimizing, just observing.

See what you learn.
