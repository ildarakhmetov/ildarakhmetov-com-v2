Let me get straight to the point:

> "In a world where AI can write your code, it's your ability to explain, connect, and communicate that sets you apart."

Now that AI can write code, refactor UIs, generate tests, and even write semi-passable documentation… what does that mean for us humans?

The big picture hasn't changed. At the core, our job is still human-centric. We write code for humans to **read**, for humans to **use**, and for humans to **collaborate** around. Even when AI tools become our teammates (or "co-pilots," if you will), we still have to communicate with them — and more importantly, with the humans behind them.

So if you work in tech — especially if you're a developer or CS student — communication skills are not optional anymore. They're essential. More than ever.

But how do you actually get better at something as vague as "communication skills"?

Here's the thing: mastering soft skills is harder than hard skills. Not because they're more complex — but because they're harder to measure, harder to practice deliberately, and harder to get feedback on.

Learning Python? Easy. Do a course, watch some videos, build a project, boom. Skill acquired. We do this all the time. We even enjoy it.

But learning to speak clearly, present an idea, or tell a compelling story in a meeting? That's a different beast. Sure, there are Udemy courses on public speaking. Books, too. (All useful — I've read some!) But let's be real — you can't learn to swim from a book. You need a pool.

Which brings me to Toastmasters.

## I joined Toastmasters this year. Should've done it earlier.

I've always wanted to. As someone who used to stutter — and somehow still chose to lecture for a living — I've had my share of rocky starts. I still remember a high school presentation where I couldn't even get through the *title* of my talk.

That's actually the story I chose for my first Toastmasters speech. (I'll tell it in full another time, I promise.)

But it took me years to finally join a club. Because showing up and speaking, as a non-native English speaker *and* someone with a past like that? That's not exactly the comfort zone.

In retrospect, though? One of the best decisions I've made in years.

Toastmasters is an organization that's over 100 years old. It was founded in California in 1924, and today has more than 16,000 clubs in over 140 countries. Think of it as a gym for your communication skills — but with timers, evaluators, impromptu speeches, and color-coded flags. And yes, it's surprisingly fun.

So, why would a software developer care?

Because when Cursor can refactor a 2,000-line React app from Material UI to shadcn in 10 minutes (true story — I went to get tea, did a few chores, and came back to a clean pull request), it's not the technical muscle that stands out anymore. It's the human stuff. The ability to explain, to persuade, to collaborate, to lead.

I've heard many techies say: "Public speaking? Meh. I sit behind three monitors. Why would I need that?"

Well, here's why:
Communication. With colleagues. With PMs. With marketing. With stakeholders. With users. With… reality.

There's a great write-up from 80,000 Hours on the kinds of skills that will become *more* valuable as AI advances. Spoiler: Communication is top of the list.

## What actually *happens* at Toastmasters?

Every club runs slightly differently, but here's what I've experienced in mine (Seymour Speakers in Vancouver — more on that later).

**1. It's structured.**
Everything is planned by the minute. Literally. There's a Timer role — someone who keeps track of every agenda item, holds up color flags to indicate time left, and even stops speakers if they go over. (Imagine someone gently holding up a red card when you ramble.)

**2. Prepared speeches.**
Most meetings have 2–3 prepared speeches, each 5–7 minutes long. Don't be fooled by the short time — people often prepare for *weeks*. It's not unusual to spend a month on one speech. It's great practice if you ever want to do a TED-style talk — or just not panic during a product demo.

**3. Evaluations (with a twist).**
Every speech is evaluated. But not casually. Each evaluation must include at least one "gift of growth" — constructive feedback to help the speaker improve. It's a safe space, but a real one. And the feedback is structured — covering everything from eye contact to body language, pitch, pace, use of filler words, and audience engagement.

There are even printable frameworks and checklists. (Yep. I love this.)

**4. Impromptu speaking (aka "Table Topics").**
This is the most fun — and the most terrifying. You're given a question on the spot and asked to speak for 1–2 minutes. No prep. No slides. Just go.

Think of it as training for those "So, what do *you* think about this, Ildar?" moments in team meetings. Can you answer clearly and compellingly — without rambling or panicking? Table Topics is the best workout for that.

**5. Filler words get tracked.**
Every single "umm," "err," "like," "you know" is counted. Yes, there's a role for that. It's eye-opening. I now notice these in **my own** speech. And… in everyone else's. Sorry, not sorry.

## So how do you join?

There are Toastmasters clubs *everywhere*. In Vancouver alone, there are dozens. It can feel overwhelming at first, but the good news is — you can visit any club for free, as a guest. Multiple times. Try a few. See what feels right.

Clubs meet at different times — mornings, lunch, evenings. I'm a morning person, so I joined one that meets at **7:30am on Thursdays** in downtown Vancouver. Perfect for me — fresh brain, quiet city. If you're curious, my club is called [Seymour Speakers](https://www.vancouvermorningtoastmasters.com/). If you're local — come join us. And if you're not, you're still welcome — our club is hybrid, and Zoom works surprisingly well.

And if 7:30am sounds like punishment, don't worry — there's a club out there for your schedule: [toastmasters.org/find-a-club](https://www.toastmasters.org/find-a-club)

## Final thought

If you're in tech in 2025, and you're thinking about one high-ROI personal improvement move… join Toastmasters.

Yes, AI can write code. But *you* still have to explain what the code does.
Yes, AI can generate slide decks. But *you* still need to pitch them.
Yes, AI can summarize docs. But *you* need to connect with people.

That human layer? That's yours. Own it.

See you at a meeting.
